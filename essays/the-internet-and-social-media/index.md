## The Immediate Responses/Reactions To:
  1. 'Traditional' journalism vs. blogs: the transition of social media as a means of getting real-world information through blogs cannot be done, but early blogs in the 90s show otherwise. Social media cannot be trusted and can never be a means of transporting reliable information.

  2. Online vs. Offline Community: Transitioning to online communities can cause a breakdown of real-world conenctions, leading to isolation and a distrust towards fellow memebers of society. On the flipside, the beginning of social media and the internet offered a way out of surveillance of governments in which people were free to choose their own identity, and create a new way of life/community. Online communities are able to create a new form of cooperation leading to altruism and revitalization of human connection/prospering positive interactions. A place for humans to get away from the hustle-and-bustle of reality. A sanctuary. 

  3. The Spread of Mis/Dis/information: Fears of faceless accounts and activity make it easy for unreliable narratives to be pushed, and never to be held accountable as it is not a 'physical' realm. 

  4. The Identity of Oneself: Social media, especially after the introduction of MySpace, offered a place for people, especially youth, to explore their identities in a 'safer' manner comapred to the real world. Youth could experiement without fear of immediate repurcussion. On the contrary however, fears of falsifying identities/fake accounts had a potential to cause harm. 

  5. The Impact(s) on 'Truth': More poeple are able to access information previously unavailable to them. People are able to be educated and educate themselves without the worries of finances (i.e. higher education) and without fear of ostracization based on who they are. Anonymity helps. However, because information is not vetted, any singular person can say whatever they like, causing a spread of misinformation. 



## But How is This Any Different From Past 'Disruptions'?
  1. Faster spread of (mis/dis) information leading to faster real-world consequences.
  2. Faceless accounts that spread such information. Probability of dishonesty at its peak
  3. Anyone is able to say anything regardless of vetting
  4. Faster dissolution of trust of institutions and collective judgements of 'truth'/knowledge/social bonds. 


## Social Media in Modernity and Its Impacts
  1. Studies have shown acceptance of information is moreso based upon feelings rather than scientific data. Information is now pushed by algorithms rather than users actively searching for answers leading to confirmation bias and harmful ideologies. 

  2. Disinformation and misinformation travel faster than actual information. Echo chambers are more prevalent than ever, and has caused people to isolate themselves within their own biases, creating hostile environments both online and offline. This has lead to a rise in conspiracy theories and distrust in scientifically-supported data. 

  3. Centering of social media has led to a breakdown of social trust/bonding including within family units. Social contract of trust is at its breaking point like never before. 

  4. Advertising from companies and ideologies influences information seen, and peoples responses to it. 


## Connection to AI

  1. Increased risk of developing one's own *personal* echo chamber, that can easily be littered with false or outright fabricated information.
  2. AI is able to replciate and output responses faster than humans can
  3. A faceless amalgamation of data retrieved from human user input, both to AI and from the internet itself, creating a false sense of 'personhood'. Isolation. 

## How I Used AI for This Essay

This essay was researched and written with the assistance of Claude, an AI assistant made by Anthropic.

AI was useful for: generating an initial outline, suggesting relevant historical figures and events, and helping draft transitions between sections. I used it as a starting point—a well-read research assistant who could quickly surface names and dates and connections.

AI was *not* a reliable source for specific claims, quotations, or bibliographic details. Every source cited here was verified through actual databases (JSTOR, Google Scholar, library catalogs) or the original texts. Several sources that an AI initially suggested turned out to have incorrect publication dates, wrong page numbers, or—in two cases—to not exist in the form described. AI is not a substitute for source verification; it is a starting point for research, not an endpoint.

The deeper limitation: AI flattened the historiographic debates. When I asked it to explain "the significance of Wikipedia," it gave a confident, balanced-sounding answer that missed exactly the tensions and contradictions that make the history interesting. Historians argue. They disagree. They revise. AI tends to synthesize and harmonize. The most important intellectual work in this essay—deciding what the evidence actually *means*—was mine to do.
